Twinkling Lights

Why do stars twinkle? The physics of stellar scintillation: atmospheric turbulence, Kolmogorov spectra, and intensity fluctuations.

Atmospheric turbulence

Light from a distant star arrives as a plane wave, but passes through the turbulent atmosphere before reaching the eye. Random refractive-index fluctuations $n(\mathbf{r}) = \bar{n} + \delta n(\mathbf{r})$, driven by temperature inhomogeneities, distort both the phase and amplitude of the wave.

Kolmogorov turbulence spectrum

In the inertial sub-range $\ell_0 \ll r \ll L_0$, Kolmogorov's theory predicts that the structure function of the refractive-index fluctuations follows a power law:

$$D_n(r) \equiv \langle [\delta n(\mathbf{r}_1) - \delta n(\mathbf{r}_2)]^2 \rangle = C_n^2\, r^{2/3}$$

where $C_n^2$ is the refractive-index structure parameter (typically $10^{-16}$–$10^{-13}$ m$^{-2/3}$ near the ground) and $r = |\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|$.

Phase perturbation and the Fried parameter

After propagating through a turbulent layer of thickness $\delta h$, the wavefront acquires a phase perturbation $\phi(\mathbf{x})$ with structure function

$$D_\phi(r) = 6.88\left(\frac{r}{r_0}\right)^{5/3}$$

where the Fried parameter $r_0 = \left(0.423\,k^2 \sec\zeta \int C_n^2(h)\,dh\right)^{-3/5}$ characterises the coherence length of the atmosphere. Typical values at a good site are $r_0 \sim 10$–$20$ cm at visible wavelengths.

Intensity scintillation

Amplitude fluctuations arise from propagation after the phase screen (Fresnel diffraction). For a thin screen at height $H$, scintillation is significant when the Fresnel scale $r_F = \sqrt{\lambda H}$ lies in the inertial range of the turbulence. The scintillation index is

$$\sigma_I^2 = \frac{\langle I^2 \rangle - \langle I \rangle^2}{\langle I \rangle^2} \approx 2.24\,k^{7/6}\,\sec^{11/6}\!\zeta \int C_n^2(h)\,h^{5/6}\,dh$$

For a star near the horizon ($\zeta$ large) $\sigma_I^2 \gg 1$ — the star twinkles violently. Planets, with finite angular diameter, average over many coherence cells and barely scintillate.
