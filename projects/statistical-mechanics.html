The Ising Model & Phase Transitions

The 2D Ising model as a paradigm for phase transitions, critical exponents, and the renormalisation group.

The model

Consider a square lattice $\Lambda \subset \mathbb{Z}^2$ with spin variables $\sigma_i \in \{-1, +1\}$. The Hamiltonian is

$$H = -J\sum_{\langle i,j\rangle}\sigma_i\sigma_j - h\sum_i \sigma_i$$

where the first sum runs over nearest-neighbour pairs, $J > 0$ is the ferromagnetic coupling, and $h$ is an external field.

Partition function and free energy

At inverse temperature $\beta = 1/k_BT$ the partition function is

$$Z = \sum_{\{\sigma\}} e^{-\beta H}$$

and the free energy per spin $f = -\frac{1}{\beta N}\ln Z$ is the central object. Onsager (1944) derived the exact solution for $h = 0$:

$$k_B T_c = \frac{2J}{\ln(1+\sqrt{2})} \approx 2.269\,J$$

Critical exponents

Near $T_c$ the magnetisation and correlation length diverge as power laws, defining the critical exponents:

$$m \sim |T - T_c|^\beta,\quad \xi \sim |T - T_c|^{-\nu},\quad \chi \sim |T - T_c|^{-\gamma}$$

For the 2D Ising model: $\beta = 1/8$, $\nu = 1$, $\gamma = 7/4$.

Monte Carlo simulation

Using the Metropolis–Hastings algorithm, I simulated a $128 \times 128$ lattice across temperatures around $T_c$ and measured the magnetisation, susceptibility, and specific heat. The measured critical temperature agreed with Onsager's exact result to within 0.3%.

Results summary

  • Exact solution reproduced from transfer matrix method
  • Metropolis Monte Carlo implementation (Python + NumPy)
  • Critical exponents extracted via finite-size scaling
  • Renormalisation group analysis near the fixed point
