Circular Binaries Mass Transfer

Roche lobe geometry, mass transfer rates, and orbital evolution in circular binary star systems.

The restricted three-body problem

A close binary is modelled as two point masses $M_1$ and $M_2$ in a circular orbit of separation $a$ and angular velocity $\Omega = \sqrt{G(M_1+M_2)/a^3}$. In the co-rotating frame an effective potential (the Roche potential) governs the structure of the gas:

$$\Phi_R(\mathbf{r}) = -\frac{GM_1}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_1|} - \frac{GM_2}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_2|} - \frac{1}{2}\Omega^2|\mathbf{r}|^2$$

Roche lobe and L1 point

The Roche lobe of each star is the region of space in which material is gravitationally bound to that star. The two lobes share a saddle point — the inner Lagrange point $L_1$ — where gas can flow between the stars. A useful approximation for the Roche lobe radius is the Eggleton formula:

$$\frac{R_L}{a} = \frac{0.49\,q^{2/3}}{0.6\,q^{2/3} + \ln(1+q^{1/3})}, \qquad q = \frac{M_1}{M_2}$$

Mass transfer begins when the donor star fills its Roche lobe (overflow).

Mass transfer rate

The transfer rate $\dot{M}$ depends on how far the donor overflows its Roche lobe. For thermal-timescale overflow:

$$\dot{M} \sim -\frac{M_{\rm donor}}{\tau_{\rm KH}}, \qquad \tau_{\rm KH} = \frac{GM^2}{RL}$$

where $\tau_{\rm KH}$ is the Kelvin–Helmholtz timescale. The sign of $\dot{J}_{\rm orb}$ determines whether mass transfer is stable or leads to runaway (common-envelope evolution).

Orbital evolution

Conservation of total angular momentum $J = J_{\rm orb} + J_{\rm spin}$ with mass loss from the system gives

$$\frac{\dot{a}}{a} = -2\frac{\dot{M}_1}{M_1} \left(1 - q\frac{R_L}{a}\right) - 2\frac{\dot{J}_{\rm loss}}{J_{\rm orb}}$$

The orbit may widen or shrink depending on the mass ratio and the efficiency of angular momentum loss (gravitational waves, magnetic braking).
